Research

Where ferns live, how they work, and how they survive.

Daniela's research uses ferns and lycophytes to ask how physiology, water availability, life cycle structure, and environmental history shape plant diversity. The work spans global richness patterns, comparative gas exchange, stomatal regulation, and strategies for persisting through drought and desiccation.

Theme 1

Biogeography and evolution of fern and lycophyte diversity

Fern and lycophyte diversity is shaped by the interaction between regional species pools, local environmental conditions, and the life-cycle structure that distinguishes these plants from seed plants. Daniela's work in this area examines richness patterns from global plot datasets to tropical elevational transects, asking when diversity reflects environmental filtering and when it reflects the size and structure of the available species pool. This theme also includes the evolutionary significance of independent gametophyte and sporophyte generations, which can experience different ecological pressures and may contribute differently to persistence and distribution.

Selected work

  • Global fern and lycophyte richness explained
  • Functional diversity in ferns is driven by species richness rather than by environmental constraints
  • The power of independent generations in plants

Theme 2

Functional and physiological ecology of ferns

Ferns and lycophytes offer a broad comparative system for testing assumptions built from angiosperm physiology. Daniela's research has examined stomatal conductance, photosynthetic efficiency, light responses, and circadian regulation across phylogenetically diverse species. These studies show that many seedless vascular plants rely more strongly on light-driven and passive water-status responses than on the rapid stomatal regulation familiar from seed plants, while some lineages such as Marsileaceae and Equisetum show distinctive physiological innovations.

Selected work

  • Exclusive and hypersensitive stomatal control by blue-light in Equisetum
  • Light, rather than circadian rhythm, regulates gas exchange in ferns and lycophytes
  • Exploring the ecological relevance and variability of circadian regulation in Marsileaceae
  • Water-Carbon Relations in Ferns

Theme 3

Desiccation tolerance and microhabitat refugia

Water availability is central to fern ecology, but drought response cannot be reduced to a single trait. Daniela's work separates poikilohydry, the inability to regulate plant water content, from desiccation tolerance, the ability to recover after drying. Recent research also tests whether humid microhabitats can buffer fern communities along aridity gradients, showing that local refugia can support richness in humid to semihumid regions but may lose that effect toward dry extremes. Together, these studies clarify how physiological syndromes and habitat heterogeneity shape fern persistence under changing climates.

Selected work

  • The refuge effect of humid microhabitats for ferns decreases towards more arid regions
  • Untangling poikilohydry and desiccation tolerance
  • In situ observations of the gametophytes of six fern species of Costa Rica
