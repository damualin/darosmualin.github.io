Research

How do plants respond to the environment?

Daniela's research uses ferns and lycophytes to ask how physiology, water availability, and other environmental stimuli shape plant diversity. The work spans global richness patterns, comparative gas exchange, stomatal regulation, and strategies for persisting through drought and desiccation.

Theme 1

The evolution of stomatal function

Stomata are central to plant physiology, balancing carbon uptake for photosynthesis against the risk of dehydration, yet different plant lineages, and even within lineages, have evolved different strategies to control their opening and closing. Ferns and lycophytes, for example, are frequently grouped as having passive stomatal control, yet they display substantial diversity in their responses to environmental cues, reflecting differences in anatomy, hydraulics and ecological strategy. Seed plants have evolved additional layers of regulation, including hormone-mediated control and interactions with surrounding tissues, further expanding the range of possible responses. This diversity highlights that stomatal function has not followed a single evolutionary trajectory, but rather has been shaped by repeated gains, losses and modifications, allowing plants to occupy a wide range of environments and respond in fundamentally different ways to drought and light.

Theme 2

Dynamic responses in plants

My research focuses on dynamic responses in plants, particularly how species adjust to changing environmental conditions across both physiological and ecological scales. I am currently working on an SNF Mobility Postdoc project aimed at understanding the environmental triggers of CAM photosynthesis in ferns. A central goal of this work is to explore how flexible the transitions between C₃ and CAM metabolism are, and to understand how CAM can be expressed in ferns in the apparent absence of circadian regulation of stomatal aperture that characterizes many other CAM plants. Looking ahead, I will contribute to a DFG Research Unit starting at the end of 2026 that examines how epiphyte communities in the humid zone of the Galápagos respond to variation in climatic water availability. This work will compare vascular and non-vascular epiphytes, with a primary focus on non-vascular groups, to assess how different life forms respond to environmental change. Our aim is to understand how seasonal patterns of water input (garúa vs rainy season) affect epiphytes, including their distribution and physiological responses, and how epiphytes in turn change the soil water input via throughfall and stemflow. Together, these projects aim to shed light on how plants dynamically respond to environmental variability, from physiological mechanisms to community-level patterns.

Theme 3

Water availability and functional ecology

Water availability is central to fern ecology, but drought response cannot be reduced to a single trait. Daniela's work separates poikilohydry, the inability to regulate plant water content, from desiccation tolerance, the ability to recover after drying. Recent research also tests whether humid microhabitats can buffer fern communities along aridity gradients, showing that local refugia can support richness in humid to semihumid regions but may lose that effect toward dry extremes. Together, these studies clarify how physiological syndromes and habitat heterogeneity shape fern persistence under changing climates.
